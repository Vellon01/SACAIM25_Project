Chapter 1  
 
1.1 Overview / Background 
 
Dengue fever is a rapidly spreading mosquito -borne viral disease that affects millions of people 
annually, particularly in Southeast Asia, the Western Pacific islands, Latin America, and Africa. 
Caused by the dengue virus (DENV), which exists in four serotypes (DENV -1 to DENV-4), the 
disease presents a wide range of clinical manifestations —from mild flu -like symptoms to life -
threatening complications such as dengue haemorrhagic fever (DHF) and dengue shock 
syndrome (DSS). A particularly dangerous complication of severe dengue is thrombocytopenia, 
a significant drop in platelet count, which raises the risk of internal bleeding, organ failure, and 
even death. 
 
Effective management of thrombocytopenia relies heavily on timely monitoring and 
intervention. However, traditional methods for tracking platelet levels are reactive and fail to 
provide predictive insight, often leading to delayed treatment. This gap between diagnostics and 
proactive care presents an opportunity to apply data science solutions for meaningful clinical 
impact. 
 
The motivation behind this project stems from both the urgency of the healthcare challenge and 
the potential of data -driven technologies to address it. Rather than selecting a straightforward 
problem, this project was chosen precisely for its complexity and real -world relevance. It 
bridges the gap between clinical medicine and computational modeling by aiming to forecast 
platelet trends using machine learning and time series techniques. Through the analysis of 
historical clinical data, the goal is to develop a robust predictive model that can alert clinicians 
to imminent platelet drops—thus enabling timely intervention and potentially saving lives. 
 
This project is not only a valuable academic exercise but also contributes to the broader mission 
of improving healthcare delivery in resource -constrained, dengue -endemic regions. It also

offers scope for future expansion, including integration with electronic health systems, real-time 
monitoring apps, or extension to other vector -borne diseases with similar clinical patterns. In 
doing so, the work aspires to enhance the role of artificial intelligence in public health and build 
scalable, efficient, and context-aware healthcare tools. 
 
1.2 Problem statement 
 
Dengue fever is a mosquito -borne viral disease prevalent in tropical and subtropical regions, 
posing a significant public health concern. The disease manifests in a spectrum of symptoms, 
ranging from mild febrile illness to severe complications such as dengue haemorrhagic fever 
(DHF) and dengue shock syndrome (DSS). Thrombocytopenia, one of the critical complications 
of severe dengue, is a condition characterized by a drastic reduction in platelet count. A 
critically low platelet count can lead to life -threatening internal and external bleeding, requiring 
immediate medical attention. Therefore, continuous monitoring and timely intervention are 
essential to prevent severe outcomes in affected patients. 
 
Current clinical practices primarily involve reactive monitoring of platelet levels through 
periodic laboratory tests. These methods, although effective, often result in delayed 
interventions due to their inability to predict drastic changes in platelet count. As a result, 
healthcare providers face challenges in administering timely treatments, leading to increased 
morbidity and mortality among dengue patients. 
 
A data-driven approach to forecasting platelet counts can significantly enhance patient care by 
enabling early prediction of thrombocytopenia. By leveraging historical platelet count data, 
machine learning, and time series forecasting models, clinicians can anticipate severe declines 
in platelet levels and implement pre -emptive interventions. Such a predictive model would aid 
in risk stratification, allowing for timely administration of supportive treatments such as platelet 
transfusions, thereby reducing the risk of complications. 
 
This research aims to develop a platelet count forecasting model using past patient data to 
predict future trends in platelet levels. The model will provide healthcare professionals with a 
proactive tool to enhance decision-making and optimize resource allocation. By integrating data 
 
7 
 
science methodologies into clinical practice, this study seeks to bridge the gap between reactive 
and predictive healthcare, ultimately improving patient outcomes in dengue management 
 
1.3 Project Objectives 
 
The primary objective of this research is to develop a predictive model for forecasting platelet 
counts in dengue patients using historical data. By employing data -driven methodologies, this 
study aims to enhance early detection and proactive management of thrombocytopenia, thereby 
reducing the risks associated with severe dengue complications. 
 
The specific objectives of this study are as follows: 
 
1. To analyse historical platelet count trends – Examining past patient data to identify 
patterns and fluctuations in platelet levels during the course of dengue fever. 
 
2. To develop a predictive model for platelet count forecasting  – Utilizing machine 
learning and time series forecasting techniques to predict future platelet levels based on past 
trends. 
 
3. To evaluate the performance of the proposed model  – Assessing the accuracy, 
reliability, and clinical applicability of the model by comparing its predictions with actual 
patient outcomes. 
 
4. To provide an early warning system for thrombocytopenia  – Enabling healthcare 
providers to anticipate severe platelet drops and administer timely interventions, thereby 
improving patient outcomes and reducing hospital burden. 
 
 
 
By achieving these objectives, this research aims to bridge the gap between traditional reactive 
monitoring methods and proactive predictive healthcare, ultimately contributing to improved 
patient care and better management of dengue-related complications.

Chapter 2:  Literature Review 
 
2.1 Introduction 
 
Dengue fever, a mosquito -borne viral infection, remains a major public health challenge, 
particularly in tropical and subtropical regions where it causes substantial morbidity and 
mortality. Accurate and early prediction of disease severity and outbreak risk is crucial for 
improving patient outcomes and efficiently managing healthcare resources, especially in low -
resource settings. Recent advancements in data science, particularly in machine learning (ML), 
deep learning, and statistical modeling, have opened new avenues for forecasting dengue 
incidence and classifying disease severity. The literature reviewed in this section spans diverse 
methodologies and focuses, including clinical and hematological predictors such as platelet 
indices and leukocyte counts, image -based ML diagnostics, and hybrid time series models like 
ARIMAX and autoregressive neural networks. Some studies also incorporate climate variables, 
vector indices, and unsupervised learning approaches to enhance prediction accuracy. 
Additionally, parallels are drawn with diseases such as Severe Fever with Thrombocytopenia 
Syndrome (SFTS), which shares clinical similarities with dengue. Collectively, these studies 
represent the current state of the art and contribute to a comprehensive understanding of how 
computational and clinical approaches can be leveraged to improve early diagnosis, severity 
classification, and outbreak forecasting of dengue fever. 
 
2.2 Details of literature review 
 
The growing burden of dengue fever, particularly in tropical and subtropical regions, has 
prompted extensive research into methods for improving diagnosis, severity assessment, and 
outbreak forecasting. Recent literature spans a variety of methodological approaches —from 
classical statistical analysis to advanced machine learning (ML), deep learning, and image -
based diagnostics—across diverse clinical and geographical contexts. 
 
Phakhounthong et al. (2018)  conducted a retrospective study at the Angkor Hospital for 
Children in Cambodia, aiming to predict the severity of dengue in pediatric patients using 
clinical and laboratory indicators available at the time of admission. The study included 198 
 
10 
 
confirmed dengue cases and employed decision tree analysis to identify predictive factors. Key 
features evaluated included hematocrit, Glasgow Coma Score (GCS), platelet count, urine 
protein, and creatinine levels. Literature cited within the study emphasized the challenge of 
differentiating dengue from other febrile illnesses and the limitations of WHO classification 
systems in clinical settings. [1]  
 
Ghosh et al. (2023)  reviewed a range of epidemiological and predictive modeling  literature, 
noting that while traditional time series models like ARIMA and its variants are widely used for 
infectious disease prediction, their accuracy improves with the addition of exogenous variables 
(e.g., rainfall). The authors build upon previous hybridization methods by combining ARIMAX 
with ARNN to model both linear and non -linear components of dengue incidence data. The 
model was tested on historical weekly dengue cases from San Juan and Iquitos, incorporating 
rainfall data as an exogenous input. It was compared against several benchmark models, 
including ARNNX, ETSX, TCNX, and TransformerX. [2] 
 
The study by Faridah et al. (2022)  conducted in Indonesia analyzed dynamic changes in 
platelet count over the course of dengue infection. Their retrospective analysis showed that 
declining platelet counts —particularly during the critical phase —correlated strongly with the 
development of dengue h aemorrhagic fever. By identifying temporal trends in platelet 
dynamics, the authors highlighted the value of serial platelet monitoring for early detection of 
severe complications. This study underscores the potential of temporal data in enhancing 
clinical vigilance and preemptive care during dengue outbreaks. [3] 
 
The study by Tanner et al. (2008) adopted a computational approach by applying decision tree 
algorithms to predict dengue diagnosis and outcomes in the early phase of illness. Using clinical 
data from patients in Vietnam, they trained and validated decision tree models capable of 
identifying key clinical features associated with confirmed dengue cases and severe outcomes. 
These features included platelet count, white blood cell count, tourniquet test results, and days 
of fever. The model demonstrated good predictive performance, supporting the use of machine 
learning techniques as an adjunct to clinical decision-making during dengue epidemics. [4] 
 
 
11 
 
Mayrose et al. (2023) proposed a machine learning framework using digital pathology to detect 
dengue from peripheral blood smear (PBS) images. The study utilized blob detection for 
platelet identification and various feature extraction techniques (morphological, GLSDM, LBP). 
Several classifiers were tested, achieving up to 95.74% accuracy with SVM. It stands out for its 
image-based ML approach, though limited by small sample size and exclusion of cytoplasmic 
features. [5] 
 
Panja et al. (2022)  introduced the XEWNet (Extended Wavelet Neural Network), combining 
wavelet decomposition (MODWT) with an ensemble neural network for dengue forecasting 
across San Juan, Iquitos, and Ahmedabad. Rainfall was found to be a causally significant 
predictor. This study surpassed traditional and deep learning models in both short -term and 
long-term forecasts. [6] 
 
Tran & Xin (2024) evaluated transformer-based architectures for long-range dengue prediction 
using multivariate climate and case data from Singapore (2000 –2019). The FWin Transformer 
(Fourier Mixed Window Attention) outperformed prior models (Autoformer, PatchTST, 
Fedformer) in both accuracy and forecasting duration. This paper offers a scalable architecture 
capable of handling non-stationary multivariate time series. [7] 
 
He et al. (2024)  addressed risk prediction in SFTS using a clinical dataset of 483 patients. 
Although focused on SFTS, the approach is directly applicable to dengue due to the clinical 
overlap. Their ML model (XGBoost) showed remarkable performance in predicting mortality, 
highlighting the role of early lab results and systemic symptoms in clinical prognosis. [8] 
 
Ong et al. (2023)  developed a dengue outbreak prediction model based on both vector indices 
and meteorological data across Kuala Lumpur districts. Their comparative analysis of seven ML 
classifiers found ensemble methods, especially XGBoost, to perform best. The Boruta algorithm 
was used to identify significant variables, revealing that the container index was the least 
important, improving the AUC by 6% upon removal. [9] 
 
 
12 
 
Silitonga et al. (2021) focused on predicting dengue severity levels (DF, DHF1, DHF2) using a 
small dataset of 77 patients from Indonesia. The study employed Artificial Neural Networks 
(ANN) and Discriminant Analysis (DA). ANN models using logistic and tanh activation 
functions achieved 90.91% accuracy, outperforming DA. This model aims to support early 
diagnosis during the third day of fever, which typically marks the transition to the critical phase. 
[10] 
 
Hair et al. (201 9) used unsupervised learning (SOM + Random Forest) to identify clinical 
patterns from 523 confirmed dengue cases in Brazil. Their clustering found four distinct groups 
correlating with disease severity, with age as the primary differentiator. Warning signs such as 
abdominal pain, hematocrit rise, and thrombocytopenia further differentiated cluster severity. 
The approach offers a novel method to uncover hidden clinical structures in dengue cases. [11]

3.3 Data Preprocessing – Extraction & preparation 
 
In order to prepare the raw dataset of dengue patients for predictive mode lling, a series of data 
preprocessing steps were conducted to transform the unstructured information into a clean, 
 
24 
 
analysable format. Initially, the dataset was standardized by renaming columns for consistency 
and forward-filling the missing values in the patient identification column.  
 
Key lab test columns such as Haematocrit, RBC, Platelet, and Haemoglobin were examined for 
non-numeric entries like “not done”, which were replaced with NaN values, followed by 
converting these columns into appropriate numerical data types. The Sex column was also 
cleaned to ensure binary categorization (i.e., only "Male" and "Female"), and all date and time 
entries were validated, cleaned, and then transformed into separate Date and Time columns 
using the datetime format. 
 
Further refinement included ensuring each patient had a unique identifier. In instances where 
the same patient number was assigned to multiple records with different age values, the records 
were split and reindexed so that each patient had a distinct ID. The Symptoms column, which 
contained free-text entries, was processed by extracting unique symptom descriptors for each 
patient, standardizing their format (e.g., capitalizing the first letter), and transforming them into 
individual binary columns, thereby creating a structured feature set.  
 
Additional features were engineered, such as days_since_onset, to quantify disease progression, 
and lag features for Platelet and Haematocrit values to help model temporal trends. Finally, 
categorical features like Sex were encoded numerically to prepare the data for machine learning 
algorithms. These preprocessing steps significantly improved the quality and utility of the 
dataset, enabling more accurate and reliable predictions.

4.4 Results & Model Accuracy  
 
After the development of the platelet prediction model using machine learning techniques (such 
as XGBoost, Random Forest and LSTM models), the system was rigorously evaluated to ensure 
its feasibility, reliability, and predictive accuracy. The model was trained on a cleaned and 
feature-engineered dataset of 493 dengue -positive patients, with the goal of forecasting platelet 
counts for the next 3 to 5 days based on early clinical indicators. 
 
Model Evaluation Metrics 
 
The predictive mode lling phase of the project involved training and evaluating multiple 
machine learning algorithms to forecast platelet counts in dengue patients. The performance of 
each model was assessed using standard regression metrics, namely Mean Absolute Error 
(MAE), Root Mean Square Error (RMSE), and the R -squared (R²) score . These metrics were 
chosen to capture both the absolute magnitude of error and the ability of the model to explain 
variability in the target variable — platelet count. 
 
Among the models tested, the XGBoost Regressor (XGBRegressor) showed robust 
performance, achieving an average MAE of 12,875.19, RMSE of 41,974.13, and an R² score of 
0.76. These results suggest that the model was relatively accurate in estimating the platelet 
trends while maintaining a good balance between bias and variance. XGBoost benefited 
significantly from the well-engineered features such as lag values, delta changes in platelet and 
hematocrit counts, and encoded symptom variables. Its gradient boosting architecture allowed it 
to model complex, non -linear relationships in the data, which is particularly important in 
clinical time-series prediction where patient responses can vary widely. 
 
In contrast, the Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) network — a deep learning model suited for 
time-series data — produced less favorable results, with an MAE of 69,104.47, RMSE of 
93,450.42, and a very low R² score of 0.07.  This indicates that the LSTM model struggled to 
capture the platelet progression trends in the given dataset. The comparatively poor 
performance of LSTM may be due to the relatively small sample size (per patient time -series), 
 
53 
 
which is often insufficient for training deep learning models that require large volumes of 
sequential data. Additionally, the variation in test intervals and sparsity in time -series data 
likely posed challenges for LSTM's memory mechanisms, which depend on regular temporal 
patterns. 
 
The Random Forest Regressor, on the other hand, demonstrated strong predictive capability, 
outperforming both XGBoost and LSTM in certain metrics. It achieved an MAE of 14,658.77, 
RMSE of 33,371.79, and an R² score of 0.83,  making it the best performer in terms of variance 
explanation. The ensemble nature of the Random Forest allowed it to handle feature interactions 
and outliers effectively, and it was less sensitive to noise in the data. Despite having a slightly 
higher MAE than XGBoost, its significantly lower RMSE and superior R² suggest it was more 
consistent and reliable in predicting sharp drops or rises in platelet counts — a critical factor in 
managing dengue progression. 
 
Overall, the comparison highlights that tree-based ensemble methods (Random Forest and 
XGBoost) are more suitable for the nature of this clinical data compared to sequence -based 
deep learning methods like LSTM. These models offer greater transparency, interpretability, 
and resilience to the irregularities present in real -world medical datasets. Based on these 
findings, the Random Forest Regressor was identified as the most feasible model for 
deployment in the Streamlit -based user interface, followed closely by XGBoost for its balance 
between speed and accuracy. 
 
4.5 Result Analysis & Discussion  
 
The results obtained from the predictive models reflect both the challenges and strengths of 
modeling platelet progression in dengue patients using clinical and laboratory data. The 
variations in performance across models can be attributed to the nature of the dataset, the 
temporal characteristics of platelet count behavior, and the modeling approach used. 
 
Among all the models tested, the Random Forest Regressor performed the best with an R² score 
of 0.83, indicating that the model could explain 83% of the variance in the platelet count values. 
 
54 
 
It also achieved a relatively low RMSE of 33,371.79 and MAE of 14,658.77, showing its ability 
to make precise and consistent predictions. This high performance can be explained by the 
ensemble nature of the Random Forest algorithm, which builds multiple decision trees and 
averages their outputs. This reduces overfitting and captures non -linear interactions between 
features such as lag values, delta changes, and symptoms — all of which are crucial for 
modeling a complex, fluctuating health indicator like platelet count. 
 
The XGBoost  Regressor also performed well with an R² of 0.76, MAE of 12,875.19, and 
RMSE of 41,974.13. While slightly less accurate than Random Forest in capturing extreme 
variations, XGBoost still demonstrated strong performance due to its gradient boosting 
mechanism, which sequentially corrects the errors of weak learners. XGBoost was especially 
effective in handling missing data and high -dimensional feature interactions, making it a 
suitable model for medical datasets with incomplete lab test results. 
 
On the other hand, the LSTM model, which was expected to perform well due to its time -series 
structure, underperformed significantly with a very low R² score of 0.07, high MAE of 
69,104.47, and RMSE of 93,450.42. This can be attributed to the limited volume of per -patient 
time-series data and the irregularity in test intervals. Deep learning models like LSTM typically 
require large and uniformly spaced temporal datasets to learn sequential patterns effectively. In 
this project, the sparse and inconsistent time steps across patients likely prevented the LSTM 
from learning meaningful trends. Additionally, LSTM is more sensitive to overfitting on small 
datasets, which may have further contributed to its poor generalization. 
 
When compared to findings from the literature review, most existing dengue prediction studies 
have focused on binary classification tasks such as severity prediction or hospitalization risk

5.1 Summary 
 
This project focused on the development of a data -driven clinical decision support system for 
predicting platelet count progression in dengue patients, using early clinical data and laboratory 
results. Recognizing the critical importance of timely platelet monitoring in dengue management, the 
system aims to assist healthcare professionals by forecasting platelet trends for the upcoming days 
— particularly during the early stages of the illness when interventions are most crucial. 
 
The project began with comprehensive data preprocessing, where raw clinical data collected from 
Fr. Muller’s Medical College Hospital was cleaned, standardized, and transformed into a structured 
dataset. This included handling missing lab results, splitting free -text symptom entries into 
structured features, engineering time -based variables like lag features and days since onset, and 
ensuring each patient record was properly indexed and sorted. The innovation here lies in the 
automated transformation of unstructured medical data into machine learning-ready format, allowing 
the models to extract meaningful patterns from complex clinical timelines. 
 
A comparative modeling approach was applied, involving XGBoost Regressor, Random Forest 
Regressor, and LSTM models. The Random Forest Regressor emerged as the most effective,

series regression approach to dengue progression prediction — an area that has received limited 
attention in research. 
 
One of the standout features of this project is the creation of an interactive, no -installation web 
interface using Streamlit, which allows users to input patient data and instantly visualize predicted 
platelet trends. This adds real -world usability to the model, making it accessible for doctors, 
students, and researchers without requiring any specialized software.

5.2 Limitations 
 
While the developed system for predicting platelet counts in dengue patients has shown 
promising results and practical utility, it is not without limitations. One of the primary 
constraints is the limited size and diversity of the dataset.  The data used for model training was 
collected from a single institution — Fr. Muller’s Medical College Hospital — and included 
493 patients. Although the dataset was rich in clinical detail, its relatively small scale and 
regional concentration may limit the generalizability of the model to broader populations across 
different geographical locations, age groups, or clinical settings. 
 
Another limitation lies in the irregularity and sparsity of time -series lab tests. Since platelet and 
hematocrit readings were not recorded at uniform intervals for all patients, time -based models 
such as LSTM underperformed. This inconsistency also posed challenges during feature 
engineering and may have affected the accuracy of the lag and delta features. A more structured 
and consistent temporal dataset would allow for deeper time -series modeling and improved 
performance of sequence-based algorithms. 
 
Additionally, the current system does not include certain clinical variables that could further 
enhance predictive accuracy, such as white blood cell count, liver enzyme levels, comorbidities, 
or medication history. The model also assumes that early lab values and symptoms alone can 
predict platelet trends, which may not capture more complex or atypical cases. 
 
From a deployment perspective, while the Streamlit interface is accessible and user -friendly, it 
remains a prototype-level tool that would require integration with hospital information systems 
(HIS) and electronic medical records (EMR) to be used in real clinical environments. Features 
like user authentication, patient data storage, real -time updates, and doctor -specific dashboards 
are yet to be implemented.

These limitations highlight the scope for future enhancements, including expanding the dataset 
across multiple hospitals, incorporating more comprehensive clinical features, exploring deep 
learning models with attention mechanisms, and developing a fully integrated clinical tool. 
Addressing these challenges will not only improve the accuracy and robustness of the model but 
also move the system closer to real-world clinical deployment. 
 
 
 
5.3 Future scope 
The current system presents a significant step toward data -driven monitoring of dengue patients 
through platelet count prediction. However, there is ample scope for future development that can 
enhance its clinical relevance, accuracy, scalability, and integration into real -world healthcare

One of the most impactful future enhancements would be the expansion of the dataset, both in 
volume and diversity. Collecting data from multiple hospitals across different regions would help the 
model learn from a wider variety of patient profiles and clinical conditions, making predictions more 
generalizable. A larger dataset would also allow the application of more advanced deep learning 
models, such as transformer -based architectures or attention -equipped LSTMs, which require 
substantial data to perform optimally. 
 
Another important direction is the inclusion of additional clinical features such as white blood cell 
count (WBC), liver function tests (LFTs), vital signs, comorbidities, and treatment history. These 
variables can offer deeper insights into disease severity and trajectory, improving the model’s ability 
to capture complex interactions that influence platelet dynamics.
